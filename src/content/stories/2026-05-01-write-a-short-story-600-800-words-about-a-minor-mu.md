The northeast quadrant was running four birds short, and Delia Marsh noticed it before she had finished her first coffee.

She stood at the corner of Fenwick and 22nd with her clicker and her clipboard, the morning traffic moving past her in its ordinary way, and counted the pigeons on the library roof three times. Eleven. She wrote it down, then stood there looking at the number.

It should have been fifteen.

Not because fifteen was arbitrary. Delia had been conducting the city's quarterly pigeon census for six years, and the library roof was a stable roost — what the methodology called a *fixed congregation point*. The overhang above the east-facing windows provided reliable thermal cover. The building super, a man named Fortis, put out birdseed in February because he felt sorry for them, which was against city code but not worth the paperwork. The population there fluctuated by one or two, sometimes three, based on season and weather and whatever logic pigeons used to make decisions. It did not drop by four on a clear Thursday in October.

She photographed the roost and moved on. She had twenty-two points to cover by noon.

But the count nagged at her. At her next fixed point — the parking structure on Clement — she got her expected seventeen and felt no relief. She ate her lunch on a bench outside the depot and paged back through her previous entries. The library roof had been at sixteen in July, fourteen in April. Fifteen last January. Solid numbers, the kind that told you a roost was healthy and stable.

Four missing birds was not a disaster. She was aware of this. But it was her job to know the difference between a population in ordinary fluctuation and one responding to a change in conditions, and four missing birds was, by any reasonable field standard, a change in conditions.

After lunch she went back.

She stood across the street and used her binoculars, which her supervisor Glenn called "a bit much" every time he saw them. Glenn conducted his own bird counts from his car window, with a pen he sometimes forgot to uncap. Delia did not say anything about this.

Through the binoculars she worked along the roofline, then dropped her gaze to the building's face. The second-floor windows. The decorative stonework. And then the small concrete ledge above the main entrance, which was not technically part of the roost and which she had never counted because birds didn't use it.

Four pigeons sat on the ledge in a tight, patient row.

Delia lowered the binoculars. She crossed the street and stood on the library steps and looked up at them. They looked down at her with the standard pigeon expression, which communicated nothing.

She looked at the door. There was a small paper sign taped to the inside of the glass: RENOVATION IN PROGRESS — EAST WING TEMPORARILY CLOSED. The sign looked like it had been there a few days.

She went inside and found the circulation desk and asked if there had been any recent work done above the east windows.

The librarian, a young man with reading glasses pushed up on his head, thought about it. "The gutter guys came Monday," he said. "They put some mesh up. Pest management, I think. I don't really know, the building department arranged it."

"Okay," said Delia.

"Is there a problem?"

"No," she said. "Thank you."

She went back outside. The four birds were still on the ledge. They were using it because the overhang was gone — blocked or covered, their usual thermal shelter inaccessible. They had moved twelve feet down and found the next available surface. It was not complicated. It was just what they had done.

She updated her notation in the margin: *4 displaced to main entrance ledge, mesh installation east overhang, confirm with bldg dept, monitor next quarter.*

Then she stood there for a moment, looking at them.

Four birds that had lost their spot and found another one. They didn't seem bothered. They had the look of creatures who had worked something out.

She clicked her pen closed and walked to her next point.
