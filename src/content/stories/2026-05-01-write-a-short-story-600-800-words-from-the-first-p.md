  Write a short story (600–800 words) from the first-person POV of an everyday object.  ## The object You choose — but pick something usually overlooked (a doorstop, a paperclip, the third button on a remote, a single sock, a fridge magnet). Avoid the obvious choices (teddy bear, diary, mirror).  ## Premise The object has opinions, preferences, and a rich inner life its humans know nothing about. Something small happens today that matters enormously to the object — a near miss, a small betrayal, a quiet triumph, a long-awaited reunion with another object.  ## Requirements - Strong, specific voice from sentence one — the object should feel like a person with a personality, not a gimmick - Include the object's theory about humans (they should be charmingly wrong about something) - At least one rivalry or friendship with another nearby object - A sense of time that isn't human (measured in dishwasher cycles, sock pairings, Tuesdays, etc.) - End on a small, satisfying moment — not a punchline  ## Avoid - Winking at the reader - Excessive cuteness - The object explaining that it's an object - Any version of "and then I got thrown away" as the ending  ## Tone Warm, observant, slightly wry. I have occupied this particular corner of the junk drawer for forty-seven Tuesday garbage days, which is how I measure permanence. Things that survive forty-seven Tuesdays are here to stay. The expired coupons didn't make it past three. The broken corkscrew held out for nineteen before being relocated to the true death—the bin beneath the sink.

I am a key. Not the house key or the car key—those arrogant brass princes who spend their days in pockets and on hooks, clinking importantly. I am smaller, silver, with a round bow and short shaft. I open something, or did once, though what exactly has been forgotten by everyone including myself.

My drawer-mate, a single AA battery with corroded terminals, insists I'm a luggage key. "You've got that TSA look about you," he says, though he's been dead for at least thirty Tuesdays and I don't trust his judgment. The refrigerator magnet shaped like Ohio—wedged sideways between the takeout menus—claims I'm clearly a diary key, which is absurd. I have none of the decorative pretensions diary keys possess.

The truth is, I don't remember. But I've developed a theory about humans that explains this: they change their minds about what matters approximately every three dishwasher cycles. One moment they're saving something precious in this drawer—a key, a battery, a rubber band ball the size of a child's fist—and four cycles later, they've forgotten why any of it mattered at all. It's a peculiar way to live, constantly renegotiating your relationship with permanence.

My only real companion is a button. Not attached to anything, naturally. She's mother-of-pearl, four holes, with the faintest pink iridescence. Elegant. We don't speak much—she's from the back of the drawer where things tend toward contemplation rather than conversation—but there's an understanding between us. We are both fasteners, both designed to keep things together, both removed from our original purpose.

For thirty of my forty-seven Tuesdays, she's been my fixed point.

Then today—a regular Thursday, mid-morning—it happened.

The drawer opened. The Hand descended. This occurs sometimes: a frantic search for batteries, rubber bands, the pizza place menu. We've all learned to brace for the stirring. But today the Hand came with purpose, methodical, moving items aside with unusual care.

"They're organizing," the battery hissed. "This is it. The purge."

But no. The Hand found what it sought: a small wooden box from the back corner. The button's section.

I felt her lifted, along with several of her kind—browns, blacks, one other pearl. The Hand carried them away, and the drawer closed.

Silence. Even the rubber band ball, usually chipper, said nothing.

Seventy-three seconds later (I'm excellent with short durations), the drawer opened again. The Hand returned the wooden box. But when it withdrew, the button remained on top of a stack of expired warranties, near the front.

Near me.

"Well," she said, after a moment. "That was unexpected."

"What happened?"

"The coat. The good wool one. It's been in the closet for—I don't know—seasons beyond counting. Its third button was hanging by a thread. The human noticed."

"So you're..."

"I'm back on active duty." There was something in her voice I couldn't quite identify. Pride, perhaps. Or fear. "Strange, isn't it? To be remembered?"

I thought about this. About purpose and permanence. About the difference between being saved and being forgotten in the same dark space.

"You'll do well," I told her. "You're very sturdy."

"Thank you." A pause. "You'll still be here, though? When they put the coat away again, and I return?"

"Yes," I said, and meant it. "I'm not going anywhere."

The drawer is quiet now in the afternoon light that leaks through the cracks. The battery has gone back to muttering about his glory days. The rubber band ball is somehow snoring.

But the button and I, we're both awake, listening to the sounds of the house settling around us, fasteners who've learned that sometimes keeping one thing together is enough.
