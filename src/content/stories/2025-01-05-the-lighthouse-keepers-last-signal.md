The light had been turning for forty-three years when Maren Soelberg first heard the voice.

She didn't call it a voice, not at first. The sound came in on the old VHF radio—the backup unit she kept on the shelf more out of habit than necessity—as a pattern of tones she didn't recognize. Not Morse. Not any distress protocol she'd trained on. Something else entirely, arriving in the gaps between the storm's own howling.

Outside, the November sea was doing what November seas do on that particular stretch of the Norwegian shelf: punishing everything. Waves cleared the breakwater and shattered against the base of the tower in explosions of white that she could feel through the soles of her boots, three floors up, even through the iron spiral staircase. She had stopped worrying about ships an hour ago—no skipper alive or suicidal would be out in this—and had turned her attention to the log, to the kettle, to the ordinary business of being the only person within forty kilometers who was not asleep.

Then the radio spoke.

She crossed the room in four steps, turned the squelch knob, held her breath. The tones resolved—almost. She had a memory of her grandmother humming to herself while peeling potatoes, a private song with no name, and the sound on the radio was like that: private, tuneless, not designed for an audience.

She pressed record on her phone out of professional reflex and listened.

It lasted eleven minutes. She knew because she checked the timestamp afterward—afterward, when the meaning of what she had heard was already beginning to dissolve the way dreams do in the first conscious moments of morning.

She could not have said what the signal meant. But she understood it. That was the terrifying part. Not with her trained ear or her keeper's knowledge of maritime bands; she understood it the way you understand that someone in the next room is crying even if you can't make out the words. Something was out there. Something had been out there for a very long time. And for eleven minutes on a November night, it had looked up and noticed the light.

When she played back the recording, she heard only static.

She checked the radio log—procedure required it. There was nothing in the frequency band she'd been listening to, nothing scheduled, nothing that should have been there. She wrote *equipment anomaly, static on backup VHF, no distress traffic* and signed the entry with her initials.

The storm passed before dawn. By morning the sea had the exhausted, polished look it gets after violence, and the rocks gleamed under a sky so pale it was nearly white. Maren climbed to the lantern room and watched the light extinguish itself automatically as the sun came up, the great Fresnel lens going still and dark, its work done.

She stood there longer than she needed to.

The thing about the light—what she had learned in forty-three years of keeping it—was that it worked in both directions. A lighthouse does not only warn ships away from rocks. It also tells something in the darkness that there is someone here, that this small point of the coast is known and tended, that a human being is paying attention.

She had always found that thought comforting.

Standing in the cold lantern room with the recording on her phone that contained only static, she turned it over in her mind: the light goes out, the light goes on. Goes out. Goes on. Forty-three years of saying *I am here*.

She wondered, and immediately regretted wondering, whether something had finally answered.

She went downstairs. She made coffee. At the appointed time, she radioed in her morning report—weather, sea state, equipment status, all normal—in her clear, unhurried voice, the voice of someone who has kept watch for a long time and intends to keep it a while longer.

Outside, the sea moved the way it always moves: without purpose, without pause, saying nothing.

Or nothing she could hear.
