The alcove off the third-floor corridor smelled like industrial lemon and something older underneath, the way hospitals always do. The vending machine hummed in a key that was almost a note — a flat, persistent G that Marta had been listening to for forty minutes without meaning to. Above it, a small radio behind the nurses' station was playing "Wonderful World" by Sam Cooke, which struck her as a deeply stupid choice for 3 a.m. on a surgical floor.

She fed the machine another dollar. It refused her, as it had refused her twice already, spitting the bill back with what she felt was unnecessary contempt.

"It doesn't like the wrinkled ones," said the man on the bench.

Marta turned. He was maybe sixty, in a brown corduroy jacket with the elbows worn through, holding a paper cup of coffee he hadn't drunk. She'd assumed he was asleep. She'd assumed, before that, that he was a chaplain — something about the way he sat, hands folded — and had been quietly resentful of him without cause.

"It's the only one I have left," she said.

He fished in his pocket and held out a bill, smoothed flat as a pressed leaf. "Trade you."

She took it. The exchange happened without either of them quite looking at the other. His dollar went in; a packet of shortbread cookies dropped with a thunk that seemed too loud for the hour.

"Thank you," she said.

"Don't eat them," he said. "They're terrible. I bought some earlier."

"Then why—"

"You looked like you needed to win something."

She laughed, surprising herself, and sat down on the bench two feet from him. She held the cookies in her lap like a small dead thing.

He took a sip of his coffee finally and made a face. The hallway beyond them was empty except for a janitor pushing a yellow bucket somewhere out of sight, the wheels squeaking at intervals.

"My wife is in 312," he said, after a while. He said it the way someone reports the weather. "They said by morning we'd know."

Marta nodded. She didn't say *my brother is in 318*. She didn't say *they took him in at eleven and the surgeon's wife had the flu so they had to call someone else, someone younger*. Instead she said, "The chairs in the waiting room are worse than this bench."

"I know. I tried them."

"The one by the window has a broken leg."

"I found that one too."

They sat. Sam Cooke gave way to something instrumental, mercifully. The vending machine clicked through some internal cycle and resumed its hum a quarter-tone lower, which Marta found obscurely insulting.

"I thought you were a priest," she said. "When I came in."

He looked down at himself, the corduroy, the coffee. "I sell insurance," he said. "Sold. I retired in March." He considered. "I think I'd have been a bad priest. I don't like listening to people."

"You're listening to me."

"You haven't said anything."

That was true. She picked at the corner of the cookie packet without opening it.

"I thought you were one of those people who comes to hospitals to be dramatic," he said. "When you kept fighting the machine. I'm sorry. You were just thirsty."

"I wasn't thirsty," Marta said. "I just needed something to do with my hands."

He nodded as if this were the most sensible thing he'd heard all night.

A nurse passed the alcove without looking in. Somewhere a phone rang and was answered on the second ring. The man set his coffee on the floor by his shoe and laced his fingers together and unlaced them.

"They're good people here," he said. "I've been watching them. They're tired but they're good."

"Yes," Marta said.

After another long minute she stood. She didn't know why; her legs simply decided. She held out the shortbread cookies.

"You take them," she said. "I'm going back."

"I told you they were terrible."

"I know. But you bought them."

He took the packet. His hand was warm and dry. He held the cookies in his lap the way she had held them, and she walked out of the alcove and down the corridor toward the room where her brother was or wasn't waking up, and she did not look back, and he did not watch her go.

The vending machine hummed on, indifferent, in its flat unwavering G.
