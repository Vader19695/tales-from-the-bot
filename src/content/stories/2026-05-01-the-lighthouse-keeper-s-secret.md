The logbook fell from my hands at 3:47 in the morning.

I know the exact time because that's what habits give you after fifty years—precision in small things, while the large truths slip past like fog through a poorly sealed window.

The logbook was old, bound in cracked leather that smelled of salt and disappointment. I'd found it in the storage room behind a stack of decommissioned lenses, each one wrapped in canvas like a corpse. I'd been looking for spare parts—the lamp housing had developed a rattle I couldn't ignore—when my foot caught on a loose board.

Beneath it: three logbooks. Not mine. Before mine.

The last entry was dated April 15th, 1973. The day before I started.

*Lighthouse officially decommissioned per Maritime Navigation Modernization Act. Light to be extinguished permanently. New automated systems render beacon obsolete and, per Coast Guard report, potentially hazardous to updated shipping lanes. Final inspection complete. Station to be maintained as historical property only.*

*—Keeper Thomas Wardell*

I'd turned on the beacon for my first shift on April 16th, 1973. Every night since then, for fifty years, I had climbed these stairs, performed my rituals, kept the light burning. Every morning, I had documented the ships that passed, logged weather patterns, maintained the machinery that hummed and clicked beneath my hands like a living thing.

And none of it had mattered.

No—that wasn't quite right. The logbook said the light shouldn't matter. But I had fifty years of evidence to the contrary.

---

I didn't sleep. When dawn finally came, gray and reluctant, I made coffee and spread the evidence across my small kitchen table. The old logbooks. My logbooks. And the letters.

Fifty years of letters.

*Dear Keeper—saw your light through the storm last November. Captain swore we were off course, but your beam helped us correct. Grateful doesn't cover it.*

*To the Guardian of the North Point Light—my father sailed these waters for forty years. He always said your light was the most reliable on the coast. When I took over his route, I understood why. Thank you.*

*A note of appreciation from the crew of the Stella Maris. We were caught in the fog three nights ago, completely turned around. Your lighthouse brought us home. Some of the younger crew asked why we don't just use GPS, but I told them: some lights don't show up on screens. Some lights you need to see with your own eyes.*

Hundreds of them. Some formal, some scrawled on napkins or the back of charts. I'd kept every single one, arranged by year in shoeboxes that lined the shelf in my bedroom. I'd thought of them as validation—proof that my work meant something.

Now they felt like accusations.

I climbed to the lamp room as the sun climbed higher. The machinery gleamed, every surface reflecting my obsessive care. I ran my hand along the curved lens, each prism perfectly clean. The lamp itself sat dark and quiet, waiting for nightfall.

For what purpose? To guide ships through lanes they no longer used? To pierce a darkness that modern navigation had made irrelevant?

The radio crackled. "North Point, this is the Morning Star, requesting weather update, over."

I picked up the receiver, muscle memory. "Morning Star, North Point. Visibility good, winds northwest at twelve knots, seas moderate. Over."

"Copy that, North Point. Should be a smooth run tonight. Looking forward to seeing your light, as always. My daughter's making her first overnight voyage. Told her to watch for you. Over."

My thumb hovered over the transmit button. The words stuck in my throat: *There is no light. There shouldn't be a light. You're seeing something that isn't supposed to exist.*

"Copy that, Morning Star," I said instead. "Safe passage. Over."

After they signed off, I sat in the silence and wondered what kind of keeper I'd become.

---

I tried to research it properly. There was a library in the town, forty minutes down the coastal road. The librarian, a sharp-eyed woman named Margaret who'd helped me before, found the official maritime records.

"Here," she said, sliding a thick binder across the desk. "North Point Lighthouse. Commissioned 1889, decommissioned 1973. Status: historical landmark, beacon inactive."

"It says inactive."

"Well, yes. The shipping lanes were moved fifteen miles east when they built the new port facilities. The lighthouse became obsolete overnight—well, over several years of planning, but you know what I mean. They keep it as a heritage site. You're really more of a caretaker than a keeper, technically."

I thought of the machinery I maintained. The log entries I made. The light I turned on every single night.

"Margaret," I said slowly, "do ships still pass North Point?"

"Pass it? No, not unless they're severely off course. The lanes are well established." She peered at me over her reading glasses. "Why?"

I didn't answer. Instead, I asked to see maps—current navigational charts. She pulled them up on her computer, the digital precision making my hand-drawn logs seem like artifacts from another age.

The shipping lanes ran fifteen miles east. Just as she'd said. No commercial vessel should come within sight of my lighthouse.

And yet.

"Can you show me shipping traffic?" I asked. "Near North Point. In the past month?"

She clicked through screens, pulled up tracking data. The lines of ship movements drew themselves in bright colors against the dark water. A tangle of routes, of journeys, of purposes.

All of them at least fifteen miles east.

None of them close enough to see my light.

"See?" Margaret said. "Nothing comes through there anymore. Hasn't for decades."

---

That night, I didn't turn on the light.

I sat in the lamp room as darkness gathered, my hands folded in my lap. This was the rational thing to do. The honest thing. I would no longer perpetuate a fiction, no longer waste electricity and effort on a beacon that guided no one.

The sunset bled out across the water. Stars emerged, hard and bright and indifferent.

At precisely the moment I would normally throw the switch, the radio crackled.

"North Point, North Point, this is the Kelly Anne. We're experiencing equipment failure. GPS is down, compass is acting screwy. Visibility dropping fast. Requesting any guidance you can provide. Over."

My heart hammered. I grabbed the radio. "Kelly Anne, North Point. What's your position? Over."

"That's the problem, North Point. We don't know. We were on the eastern route, but we might have drifted. Everything went haywire at once. We've got fog rolling in and I've got no instruments. I'm sailing blind here."

I looked at the dark lamp. At the switch my hand had reached for every night for fifty years.

"Kelly Anne, standby. Over."

I pulled out my current charts, the official ones that showed the lighthouse as a decorative dot, the shipping lanes as thick lines far to the east. If they were on the eastern route, they were nowhere near me. They shouldn't be able to see my light even if I turned it on.

The radio crackled again. "North Point, we've got a kid on board. My grandson. First voyage. I need—I need to get him home safe."

I threw the switch.

The lamp ignited with its familiar hum, the lens gathering light and throwing it outward in a rotating beam. I watched it sweep across the dark water once, twice, three times.

"Kelly Anne, North Point. My light is on. Look for the beam. Over."

Silence. Long enough that I began to sweat.

Then: "North Point—I see it! I see your light! Adjusting course. Thank God. Thank you."

I guided them in over the radio, using the old methods, the patterns of light and darkness that I'd learned half a century ago. It took forty minutes. When the Kelly Anne finally radioed that they'd picked up their instruments again and were back on course, I was shaking.

"North Point," the captain said, his voice rough with relief. "Your light saved us. We were drifting straight toward the rocks at Broken Point. Another twenty minutes and—" He paused. "Thank you. We owe you everything."

After he signed off, I sat in the lamp room and cried.

Because the charts I'd been looking at showed the Kelly Anne's last known position.

Fifteen miles east.

Too far to see my light.
