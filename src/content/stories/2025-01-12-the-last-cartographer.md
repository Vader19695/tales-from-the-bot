The commission arrived on paper, which was the first surprise.

Yusuf had not received a paper commission in eleven years. His printer hadn't worked in six. He turned the envelope over twice before opening it, noting the handwritten address in a script that belonged to someone who had learned to write before touchscreens existed.

The letter was brief. A family had purchased a farmstead in the uplands—forty hectares, mostly forest, a creek that changed course every few winters. They had two children, ages seven and nine. They wanted a map of the property drawn by hand, at a scale of 1:2,000, on archival paper, using the conventions of classical topographic cartography. They had found him through a forum for analog craftwork that Yusuf had forgotten he'd joined. They offered a sum that was, by any contemporary standard, absurd.

He called the number in the letter. The woman who answered had a quiet voice and said her name was Petra.

"The children's grandfather was a surveyor," she said. "He died in the spring. He used to say that a map tells you where you are, but a drawn map tells you that someone was there."

Yusuf took the job.

He drove up on a Tuesday when the weather was good. Petra met him at the gate with coffee in a thermos and asked no questions about his equipment, which he appreciated. He spent two days walking the property with his old surveying compass and a notebook, taking measurements, counting paces, noting the positions of notable trees—an oak split by lightning, a birch with an unusual lean, the place where the creek bent around a limestone outcrop the size of a car.

He noted things that no satellite would record: the corner of the field where the light came in a particular way in the late afternoon, the section of path that sounded different underfoot because of what lay beneath, the small depression in the forest floor that was, without question, the remains of something built long ago by someone whose name was not available in any database.

Back in his studio, he drafted. He used his old drafting table, the one with the scratch on the surface he'd made in his first week as an apprentice, forty years ago. He used the Rapidograph pens he'd kept clean and capped in their case because he had never quite been able to throw them away.

It took three weeks.

He drew the contour lines at two-meter intervals. He drew the trees—not all of them, not with algorithmic completeness, but the ones that mattered to the shape of the place. He drew the creek twice, once as it was when he visited and once in ghost-lines showing the path it had taken before. He invented, with only slight guilt, a small symbol for the stone circle hidden in the birch grove: a cartographer's convention, a small note that says *something is here that resists description*.

He mailed the map in a cardboard tube, protected by tissue paper.

Petra called two days after it arrived. He expected thanks. Instead she said: "My daughter asked me whose footsteps those were, in the corner of the meadow."

He wasn't sure what she meant. He had drawn nothing in that corner—only the contours, the fence line, the edge of the forest.

"I think she means the way you drew the grass," Petra said. "She says it looks like someone walked through it."

Yusuf sat with that for a moment. Then he thanked her for the commission.

After the call, he sat at the drafting table for a long time. He had drawn thousands of maps. Precise maps, measured maps, maps that had navigated ships and platted cities and divided estates without error. He had never once thought about what his hands left behind.

The last cartographer—which was how the forum described him, half as joke and half as epitaph—looked down at the scratch on the drafting table.

He thought: *all maps are also portraits.*

He didn't know if that was true. But he wrote it in the margin of his notebook, capped his pens, and went to find something to eat.
